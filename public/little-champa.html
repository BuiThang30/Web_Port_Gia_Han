Little Champa

I know the Vietnamese word “chăm” (diligent) has nothing to do with the Champa people, yet every Champa person I’ve met feels like the definition of it. Their rustic resourcefulness is still alive when you walk through Central Vietnam.

The Champa came from Malaysia long ago, and with just clay and sand they built a whole empire. Now, that spirit is palpable in corners of Bàu Trúc pottery village, where women shape clay with nothing but practiced hands—no wheels, no kilns—to form cooking frames, Apsara statues, and maybe even the temples themselves. At Po Klong Garai nearby, the towers rise as if giant hands pressed them from one massive mound of clay.

So, Bau Truc clay it is. Raw and sandy from the Quao River. This work is about that diligent, “chăm” spirit and my own reflections on giving matter a kind of consciousness. I called it “Little Champa” because it feels like holding an entire civilization in my hands, pocket-sized, a little clumsy, and full of my oopsies. Thanks, Bob Ross. There were no mistakes throughout this process, only happy little accidents!
